The World is Built on Two Numbers

Every calculation, every decision, and every pixel on this screen boils down to the fundamental principles of digital logic—a system built entirely on the states of 0 and 1.

The Binary Foundation

Digital logic operates on the binary system, where information is represented by one of two states: OFF (0) or ON (1). This corresponds directly to physical voltage levels in a circuit—low for 0, high for 1. This simple, robust system is the bedrock of all modern computing, from simple switches to complex microprocessors.

The Building Blocks: Logic Gates

Logic gates are elementary circuits that perform a single logical operation. While simple on their own, they are combined by the millions to create complex digital systems. They fall into several categories, with NAND and NOR gates being "Universal" because either one can be used to create any other logic function.

Constructing Circuits: The Full Adder

Combinational circuits produce an output based solely on their current inputs. A prime example is the Full Adder, which adds three bits together. This circuit is fundamental to computer arithmetic and is built by combining simpler components—in this case, two Half Adders and an OR gate.

Adding Memory & Time: Sequential Logic

Sequential circuits introduce memory. Their output depends on both current inputs and past states. Most modern systems are **synchronous**, using a clock to ensure state changes happen in an orderly fashion. **Asynchronous** circuits change state the moment an input changes, which can be faster but harder to design reliably.

From Logic to Silicon: CMOS Hardware

Logic gates are physically built from transistors. The dominant technology is CMOS, which uses complementary pairs of PMOS and NMOS transistors. PMOS transistors are used in the "pull-up network" to connect the output to HIGH (1), while NMOS transistors form the "pull-down network" to connect the output to LOW (0). This design is incredibly power-efficient.
